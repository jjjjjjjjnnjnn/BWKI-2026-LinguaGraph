Insight 1: Knowledge Builds from a Core

The concentric shell layout is not decorative — it encodes a research finding. Elementary concepts (37 nodes, green) form a dense core at the center. Each education level builds outward: 37 → 46 → 193 → 298 concepts as knowledge expands.

Elementary (37)   Middle (46)   High (193)   College (298)

Why this matters: The exponential growth (37 → 298) reflects how mathematics specializes — from basic arithmetic to dozens of advanced subfields. This "knowledge cone" pattern is visible at a glance in the 3D visualization.

🌐

Insight 2: Languages Share a Core, Diverge at the Edges

Of 574 concepts, 247 (43%) appear in all three languages. These form the mathematical "universal core" — concepts like Function, Derivative, and Integral that are taught across all curricula. The remaining 327 concepts are language-specific:

CoverageConcepts%Interpretation
ZH exclusive——Chinese curriculum depth (elementary arithmetic, probability details)
EN exclusive——Applied math, statistics, Khan Academy topics
DE exclusive——Advanced analysis, differential geometry emphasis

Why this matters: The high shared core (43%) confirms that mathematics has a language-independent foundation. But the 15% ZH-exclusive concepts show that curriculum design — not just the math itself — shapes how knowledge is organized. The Language Distance Score (LDS) will quantify these structural differences.

🔗

🔬 So what's the research contribution?

Three things that no existing system does together:

1. LDS (Language Distance Score) — A new metric that quantifies structural differences between language-specific knowledge graphs. Not translation comparison — structure comparison.

2. Cross-language knowledge graph construction — Automated pipeline from textbook text to aligned knowledge graphs across ZH/EN/DE, validated with 68 textbooks.

3. Interactive 3D comparison — CognitiveSpace lets you see the structural differences, not just compute them. Toggle languages, explore neighborhoods, trace knowledge paths.
