Chapter 12 Vectors and the Geometry of Space

12.1 Vectors

A vector v = ⟨a, b, c⟩ has magnitude |v| = √(a² + b² + c²) and direction.

Vector operations:
- Addition: u + v = ⟨u₁+v₁, u₂+v₂, u₃+v₃⟩
- Scalar multiplication: cv = ⟨ca, cb, cc⟩
- Dot product: u · v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ + u₃v₃ = |u||v|cos θ
- Cross product: u × v = ⟨u₂v₃-u₃v₂, u₃v₁-u₁v₃, u₁v₂-u₂v₁⟩

The dot product measures projection; the cross product gives a perpendicular vector.

14.4 Partial Derivatives

If z = f(x,y), the partial derivatives are:
∂f/∂x = lim_{h→0} [f(x+h,y) - f(x,y)] / h
∂f/∂y = lim_{h→0} [f(x,y+h) - f(x,y)] / h

The gradient is grad f = (∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y), pointing in the direction of steepest ascent.

Directional derivative: D_u f = ∇f · u (dot product with unit direction vector)

14.6 The Chain Rule (Multivariable)

If z = f(x,y) and x = g(t), y = h(t), then
dz/dt = ∂f/∂x · dx/dt + ∂f/∂y · dy/dt

14.8 Lagrange Multipliers

To optimize f(x,y) subject to g(x,y) = 0:
∇f = λ∇g and g(x,y) = 0

Key relationship: Partial derivatives extend single-variable differentiation to multiple variables. The gradient unifies direction, rate of change, and optimization.
