Chapter 5 Linear Transformations

5.1 Definition and Examples

A linear transformation T: V → W satisfies:
- T(u + v) = T(u) + T(v)
- T(cu) = cT(u)

Examples:
- Rotation, reflection, projection in R²/R³
- Differentiation: D(p) = p' (from Pₙ to Pₙ₋₁)
- Integration: ∫₀ˣ p(t)dt

5.2 Kernel and Image

Ker(T) = {v ∈ V : T(v) = 0} (null space)
Im(T) = {T(v) : v ∈ V} (column space)

Rank-Nullity: dim(Ker T) + dim(Im T) = dim(V)

5.3 Matrix Representation

Fix bases for V and W. T corresponds to matrix A where [T(v)]_B = A[v]_B.

Composition of transformations = matrix multiplication.

5.4 Change of Basis

If B and B' are two bases, the transition matrix P satisfies:
[x]_B = P[x]_B'

Matrix of T in new basis: A' = P⁻¹AP

Key relationship: Linear transformations are the abstract version of matrices. The matrix depends on the choice of basis; the transformation itself is basis-independent.

Chapter 7 Inner Product Spaces

7.1 Inner Products

⟨u, v⟩ = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ + ... + uₙvₙ (in Rⁿ)

Properties: symmetry, linearity, positive definiteness

7.2 Orthogonality and Gram-Schmidt

Vectors are orthogonal if ⟨u, v⟩ = 0.
Gram-Schmidt process converts any basis to an orthonormal basis.

7.3 Least Squares

Best approximate solution to Ax = b: x̂ = (A^T A)⁻¹ A^T b
Geometric interpretation: orthogonal projection onto column space.

7.4 Orthogonal Projections

Proj_W v = Σᵢ ⟨v, qᵢ⟩qᵢ where {qᵢ} is an orthonormal basis for W.

Key relationship: Inner products generalize the dot product to abstract vector spaces, enabling concepts of angle, distance, and orthogonality.
