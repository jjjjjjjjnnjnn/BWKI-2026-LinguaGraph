3.2 The Product and Quotient Rules

The Product Rule: If f and g are both differentiable, then
d/dx[f(x)g(x)] = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)

In Leibniz notation: d/dx(uv) = u'v + uv'

The Product Rule can be extended to three or more functions:
d/dx(uvw) = u'vw + uv'w + uvw'

The Quotient Rule: If f and g are both differentiable and g(x) ≠ 0, then
d/dx[f(x)/g(x)] = [f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)] / [g(x)]²

In Leibniz notation: d/dx(u/v) = (u'v - uv')/v²

Memory aid for the Quotient Rule: "Low d-high minus high d-low, all over the square of what's below"

Examples:
1. d/dx(x²e^x) = 2x·e^x + x²·e^x = xe^x(2 + x) [Product Rule]
2. d/dx(sin x / x) = (x·cos x - sin x·x) / x² = (x cos x - sin x)/x² [Quotient Rule]
3. d/dx(e^x / (1 + x²)) = (e^x(1+x²) - e^x·2x) / (1+x²)² [Quotient Rule]

Higher-Order Derivatives: If f is differentiable, its derivative f' is also a function. If f' is differentiable, we call f''(x) = d²f/dx² the second derivative. This process can continue to higher orders: f'''(x), f⁽⁴⁾(x), etc.

Physical interpretation: If s(t) is position, then s'(t) = v(t) is velocity, s''(t) = a(t) is acceleration, and s'''(t) is jerk.
