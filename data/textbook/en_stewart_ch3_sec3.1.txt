Chapter 3 Differentiation Rules

3.1 Derivatives of Polynomials and Exponential Functions

The Power Rule: If f(x) = x^n where n is a real number, then f'(x) = nx^(n-1).

This rule extends to any real exponent, not just positive integers. For example:
- d/dx(x^(1/2)) = (1/2)x^(-1/2)
- d/dx(x^(-3)) = -3x^(-4)
- d/dx(x^π) = πx^(π-1)

The Constant Multiple Rule: If c is a constant and f(x) is differentiable, then d/dx[cf(x)] = cf'(x).

The Sum and Difference Rules: If f and g are differentiable, then:
d/dx[f(x) ± g(x)] = f'(x) ± g'(x)

Derivatives of Exponential Functions:
- d/dx(e^x) = e^x (the exponential function is its own derivative)
- d/dx(a^x) = a^x · ln a

The number e is defined as the unique number for which lim_{h→0} (e^h - 1)/h = 1. This makes e^x the only function that equals its own derivative.

Applications:
- Velocity as derivative of position: v(t) = s'(t)
- Acceleration as derivative of velocity: a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)
- Marginal cost in economics: MC = C'(x)

The derivative as a rate of change connects differentiation to real-world problems in physics, biology, and economics. The tangent line approximation uses the derivative to estimate function values near a known point.

Key relationship: Differentiation is the inverse operation of antidifferentiation (integration). The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects these two operations.
