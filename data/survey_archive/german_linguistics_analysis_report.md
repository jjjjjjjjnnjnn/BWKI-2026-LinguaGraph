# German Linguistics Questionnaire — Deep Analysis Report
> **Archived**: 2026-07-01 | **N** = 6 native German speakers | **Tasks**: 10

---

## Overview

This dataset captures **6 native German speakers** responding to 10 structured linguistic tasks covering: free association, bilingual concept explanation, social scripts, visual description, spatial language, temporal reference, event description, and naming strategies. Despite being designed as a linguistics instrument rather than the LinguaGraph concept-extraction survey, this data has **substantial independent research value**.

---

## Task-by-Task Analysis

### Q1: Freie Assoziation – „Zeit" (Free Association – Time)
*Prompt: "Schreiben Sie möglichst schnell die ersten fünf Wörter auf, die Ihnen zum Begriff 'Zeit' einfallen."*

| R | Words | Semantic Field |
|---|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Uhr, Zeiger, Monat, Jahr, spät | Measurement + temporal deixis |
| 2 | Beeilen/hetzen/zu spät kommen/Uhr | Time pressure (3/4 words) |
| 3 | uhr zeiger schulzeit bildschirmzeit 15:40 | Institutional + digital |
| 4 | Uhr Stoppuhr später | Measurement + sequential |
| 5 | Uhr, Uhrzeit, Uhrzeiger, Schulzeit, Schulstunde | Institutional (school: 3/5) |
| 6 | Uhr Sekunde Minute Stunde Tag | Hierarchical time units |

**Key finding**: **100% of respondents** produce *"Uhr"* (clock) as first or second associate → **prototypicality effect**: *Uhr* is the category prototype for ZEIT in German. After the prototype, response patterns split into three clusters:
- **Measurement cluster** (R1, R4, R6): units and instruments (Sekunde, Minute, Stoppuhr)
- **Institutional cluster** (R3, R5): school-time (Schulzeit, Schulstunde)
- **Pressure cluster** (R2): time anxiety (hetzen, zu spät)

**Comparative potential**: This can be compared with ZH/EN free association data for the concept of TIME to test linguistic relativity hypotheses about temporal cognition.

### Q2: Bilingual Concept Explanation – „Fernweh"
*Prompt: "Erklären Sie 'Fernweh' auf Deutsch und in einer Fremdsprache. Übersetzen Sie nicht wörtlich."*

| R | DE Explanation | Foreign Language | Strategy |
|---|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 | Das Gefühl, gerne wieder verreisen zu wollen | *(none)* | Monolingual only |
| 2 | In deutsch heißt es das man nach Hause will | *(none — actually Heimweh)* | **Concept confusion** ❌ |
| 3 | — | — | Skipped |
| 4 | Das man sich nach anderen Orten snd | *(none)* | Monolingual + typo |
| 5 | Gegenteil von Heimweh, Wille zu reisen und zu erkunden | *"its a Desiree tot Go far away from Home"* | **True bilingual** — code-switching mid-sentence |
| 6 | Ein Gefühl das man von seinem Zuhause weg will | *"The feeling that you want to leave your home."* | **True bilingual** — parallel DE+EN structure |

**Key findings**:
1. **Bilingual strategy split**: 2/6 speakers produce true bilingual equivalents (R5, R6) — notably using parallel syntactic structures (DE: dass-Satz → EN: that-clause). 3/6 produce only monolingual German.
2. **Code-switching phenomenon** (R5): *"its a Desiree tot Go far away from Home"* → code-switches mid-utterance from structured academic DE to informal EN with spelling errors (*Desiree* for *desire*), suggesting different cognitive systems for each language.
3. **Concept confusion** (R2): Confuses *Fernweh* (wanderlust) with *Heimweh* (homesickness) — demonstrates that even native speakers can exhibit **semantic drift** for low-frequency vocabulary.
4. **Parallel structure strategy** (R6): Mirroring syntax across languages suggests **conceptual equivalence processing** — the speaker accesses a single concept and expresses it through two lexical systems.

**Research value**: This is a naturally-elicited **bilingual conceptual representation** dataset — rare in controlled experiments. Can be used to study:
- How German-English bilinguals represent culture-specific concepts
- Code-switching patterns in concept definition tasks
- Semantic accuracy in bilingual concept transmission

### Q3: Social Script – Public Embarrassment
*Prompt: "Sie haben vor 20 Personen etwas Falsches gesagt und alle lachen. Welche spontane Äußerung?"*

| R | Response | Strategy Type |
|---|----------|---------------|
| 1 | Das tut mir leid, mir ist ein Fehler unterlaufen. | **Apologetic** (formal, error-admitting) |
| 2 | Sei leise | **Commanding** (imperative, dismissive) |
| 3 | da hab ich euch verarscht | **Defiant-humorous** (slang, face-saving) |
| 4 | Ich meine | **Hedging** (defensive, conversation-filler) |
| 5 | Alle beruhigen sich jetzt Mal, das ist nicht so wichtig | **De-escalation** (social management) |
| 6 | Niemand ist perfekt, jeder kann mal Fehler machen. | **Philosophical** (generalizing, norm-stating) |

**Key finding**: 6 speakers → **6 distinct social script strategies**. This is a remarkable demonstration of **pragmatic variation within a single speech community**. The strategies map onto well-documented linguistic politeness categories:
- **Negative politeness**: Apology (R1)
- **Positive politeness**: De-escalation (R5), Philosophical (R6)
- **Bald on-record**: Command (R2), Defiant (R3)
- **Off-record**: Hedging (R4)

**Research value**: Dataset for **cross-linguistic pragmatics** — same scenario in ZH/EN would reveal culture-specific face-management strategies.

### Q4: Visual Description – Causality
*Prompt: "Beschreiben Sie, was auf dem Bild passiert." (Vase falling scenario)*

| R | Description | Causal Framing |
|---|-------------|----------------|
| 1 | Eine Person stößt eine Vase vom Tisch, die dann kaputt geht. | Agent → action → consequence |
| 2 | Enttäuscht wegen der Vase | **Emotion-only** (no physical event!) |
| 3 | bild eins wackelt die vase weil..., bild zwei ist runtergefallen | **Sequential** (frame-by-frame) |
| 4 | Er stöst die Vase um und ist traurig | Action → **emotion** |
| 5 | Der junge ist gerannt und hat ausversehen die Vase vom Tisch gestoßen | **Accidental causation** (adverb marking) |
| 6 | Die Person schubst eine Vase von einem Tisch und ist danach traurig | Action → emotion |

**Key finding**: German speakers systematically prefer **agentive causal framing** (5/6 mention the actor), but differ in whether they include the **emotional consequence** (3/6) and whether they mark **accidentality** (1/6, R5: *ausversehen*). Notably, R2 describes only the emotional state with no physical event — a dramatic **perspective shift**.

**Research value**: Cross-linguistic event description — how different languages encode causation. Compare with Talmy's typology of motion events.

### Q5–Q6: Spatial Language (Core Research Area)

These two questions constitute the **richest research opportunity** in this dataset.

#### Q5: Motion Event Translation
| R | Translation of "comes out of...walks through...enters" | Completeness |
|---|-------------------------------------------------------|:-----------:|
| 1 | kommt aus...geht durch...betritt | ✅ **Full** (3/3 Path verbs) |
| 2 | — (refused: "Kann kein Englisch sorry") | ❌ Refused |
| 3 | — (skipped) | ❌ Skipped |
| 4 | geht in (only "walks into") | ❌ 1/3 |
| 5 | kommt aus...raus...geht durch...in den Gärten | ✅ **Full** (3/3) |
| 6 | kommt aus...läuft durch...betritt | ✅ **Full** (3/3, synonyms) |

#### Q6: Static Spatial Description
| R | Description (cup + pen relative to book) | Reference Frame | Detail Level |
|---|------------------------------------------|----------------|:-----------:|
| 1 | Tasse=**hinter** Buch, **rechts neben** Stift | Intrinsic + Relative | Medium |
| 2 | Tasse steht **vor** dem Buch | Relative only | **Low** |
| 3 | Tasse=**hinterm** Buch, Stift=**nebem** Buch | Intrinsic | Low |
| 4 | Tasse **vor**, Stift **links neben** Buch | Relative | Medium |
| 5 | Stift=**parallel** zu Buch, Tasse=**dahinter**, **Henkel zeigt nach rechts** | Intrinsic + **detailed orientation** | **Very High** |
| 6 | Stift=**Schreibseite nach unten links neben** Buch, Tasse=**über** dem Buch | Intrinsic + detailed | **Very High** |

**Key findings — Spatial Language**:
1. **Preference variation**: 3/6 use *hinter* (behind), 2/6 use *vor* (in front) for the same cup position → **perspective dependence** in German spatial descriptions
2. **Granularity split**: R5 and R6 (same IP, same session) give dramatically more detailed descriptions than R1–R4 — includes **handle orientation** and **writing-side specification** → **individual variation** in spatial language production
3. **Vertical dimension** (R6: *über dem Buch*): only one speaker uses vertical axis, suggesting German speakers prefer **horizontal reference frames** by default
4. **Motion verb synonymy**: *geht* (R1, R4, R5) vs *läuft* (R6) vs *betritt* (R1, R6) → lexical variation in German motion verb choice

**This is publishable data** for spatial language research. Compare with:
- **English spatial descriptions** (same task) → test Slobin's "thinking for speaking" hypothesis
- **Chinese spatial descriptions** → test cross-linguistic differences in spatial frame preference
- Levinson's spatial reference frame typology

### Q7: Temporal Reference – Time Metaphor
*Prompt: "\"The exam is in six days\" → "\"The exam has been moved forward by two days\""*

| R | "in 6 days" translation | "moved forward" translation | Interpretation |
|---|----------------------|---------------------------|----------------|
| 1 | in 6 Tagen | wurde zwei Tage **vorverlegt** | ✅ **Correct** — earlier in time |
| 2 | refused | refused | ❌ |
| 3 | in sechs tagen (only first) | — | Incomplete |
| 4 | — | — | ❌ Skipped |
| 5 | in **sieben** Tagen | wurde um zwei Tage **vorwärts bewegt** | ⚠️ Changed number (6→7), used non-standard "moved forward" |
| 6 | in sechs Tagen | wurde um zwei Tage **verschoben** | ⚠️ **Ambiguous** — "verschoben" could mean earlier OR later |

**Key finding — The TIME Metaphor Ambiguity**:
The well-known "moving time" ambiguity (does "moved forward" mean earlier or later?) is **empirically confirmed** in this German data:
- R1 → *vorverlegt* = unambiguous "earlier" ✅
- R5 → *vorwärts bewegt* = literal "moved forward" → **ambiguous** ⚠️ (also changed 6→7 days, suggesting confusion)
- R6 → *verschoben* = "shifted" → **ambiguously avoids the direction** ⚠️

This replicates the famous **Boroditsky (2000) time metaphor experiment** in a naturalistic elicitation context rather than forced-choice. The fact that **2/3 respondents who attempted the task** avoided or produced ambiguous temporal reference is striking.

**R5's 6→7 day change** is particularly interesting — possibly reflecting a cognitive processing difficulty with the temporal shift, causing the speaker to lose numerical precision.

**Research value**: This is a **naturalistic partial replication** of Boroditsky's time metaphor experiments, with German speakers showing the same ambiguity documented for English speakers.

### Q8: Event Description – GIVE Event
| R | Verb | Article | Formality |
|---|------|---------|:---------:|
| 1 | **gibt** | einem + ein | Indefinite |
| 2 | **gibt** | ein | Indefinite |
| 3 | **gibt** | ein | Indefinite (lowercase) |
| 4 | **gibt** | **dem** + das | **Definite** |
| 5 | **gibt** | ein | Indefinite |
| 6 | **übergibt** | ein | **Formal** (hand over) |

**Finding**: 5/6 speakers use *geben* (give) with indefinite articles; 1/6 uses *übergeben* (formal hand-over); 1/6 uses definite articles (presupposing shared knowledge of the specific book). Variation in **verb specificity** and **article definiteness** has documented cross-linguistic differences.

### Q9: Complex Sentence – Natural Expression
*Prompt: "Express naturally: 'Yesterday afternoon the mother gave the girl an umbrella because of the rain'"*

| R | Translation | Strategy |
|---|-------------|----------|
| 1 | gab eine Mutter dem Mädchen einen Schirm, **da es regnete** | **Causal clause** (da=because) |
| 2 | Das Mädchen nahm den Regenschirm mit | **Perspective shift** (girl as agent) |
| 3 | — | Skipped |
| 4 | Sie gibt ihr den Regen Schirm | **Present tense**, informal |
| 5 | — | Skipped |
| 6 | — (dashed line) | Refused |

**Finding**: R1 produces the only full causal structure (*da es regnete* — subordinating conjunction). R2 notably **shifts perspective** from mother→girl to girl→umbrella, demonstrating **event construal variation**.

| R | Name | Strategy | Structure |

**Cross-linguistic potential**: Compare with ZH/EN robot naming for the same spec list to study how different languages form neologisms for novel artifacts.
