Success is the state or condition of meeting a defined range of expectations. It is the opposite of failure, with criteria varying by context, observer, or belief system. The same situation can be viewed as success by one person and failure by another.

In American culture, success is closely tied to the American Dream, representing achievement, success, and materialism. Max Weber's Protestant work ethic emphasizes hard work and deferred gratification as paths to success.

In education, student success is measured through grades. Carol Dweck's mindset theory distinguishes between fixed intelligence beliefs and growth-oriented learning, which fosters persistence toward success despite setbacks.

In business, success requires measurement against stated objectives and peer achievements. Malcolm Gladwell's Outliers argues the self-made man is a myth—entrepreneurial success owes more to circumstance than innate talent.

In science, theories succeed when predictions are confirmed by experiment. In probability, Bernoulli trials label outcomes as "success" or "failure" without moral judgment.

Despite widespread pursuit of fame and success, successful people are often unhappy. CEOs experience depression at more than double the rate of the public at large. People focus on objective benchmarks (status, wealth) rather than subjective fulfillment (self-worth, relationships), leading to disillusionment.

Key themes: success is context-dependent, observer-relative, and often measured differently across domains from biological reproduction to educational grading to business performance.
