Justice concerns treating individuals fairly. The most widely accepted core definition comes from Justinian's Institutes: "the constant and perpetual will to render to each his due." A just society is one where people receive what they deserve, though interpretations vary across ethics, law, religion, and philosophy.

Ancient Greek philosophers laid the foundation. Plato addressed justice in The Republic, defining it as balance and harmony—the right relationship between conflicting aspects within an individual or a community. Aristotle developed distributive justice (fair distribution of goods) and corrective justice. Saint Augustine argued that without justice, kingdoms are nothing but gangs of criminals.

Early modern thinkers like John Locke located justice in natural law. Jean-Jacques Rousseau championed social contract theory. Modern frameworks include distributive justice, utilitarianism, retributive justice, and restorative justice.

John Rawls argued that justice is the first virtue of social institutions, as truth is of systems of thought. His theory of justice as fairness uses the veil of ignorance to derive two principles: equal basic liberties for all, and social/economic inequalities only if they benefit the least advantaged. Robert Nozick counterargued that distributive justice concerns individual entitlement history, not matching an ideal pattern.

Distributive justice addresses what goods are distributed (wealth, power, respect, opportunities), between what entities, and what constitutes proper distribution. Key principles include equality, need, merit, and property rights.

Retributive justice holds that all guilty individuals deserve proportionate punishment. Restorative justice aims to repair harm to victims through dialogue and accountability. Utilitarian justice maximizes total welfare through deterrence, rehabilitation, and incapacitation.

Types of justice include: social justice, environmental justice, racial justice, criminal justice, procedural justice, organizational justice, and transformative justice. The UN Sustainable Development Goal 16 emphasizes strong institutions to uphold justice for all.

Equality before the law is a core principle, though Anatole France satirized that "the law forbids rich and poor alike to sleep under bridges." Proportionality holds that rewards and punishments should match the merit or gravity of actions.
