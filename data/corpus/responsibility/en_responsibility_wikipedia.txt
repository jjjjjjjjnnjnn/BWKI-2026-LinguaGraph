Moral responsibility is the status of deserving praise, blame, reward, or punishment for actions based on one's moral obligations. Those who bear this status are moral agents—beings capable of reflecting on situations, forming intentions, and acting on them.

The concept is distinct from legal responsibility, though they often overlap. Philosophically, moral responsibility requires two conditions: the control (freedom) condition concerning free will, and the epistemic condition concerning awareness of the action, its moral significance, and its consequences.

Key philosophical positions include: metaphysical libertarianism (free will exists, determinism would eliminate it), hard determinism (free will is denied, requiring a different moral framework), hard incompatibilism (free will is impossible whether actions are determined or random), and compatibilism (determinism and free will can coexist).

Thomas Nagel identified four types of luck affecting moral evaluation, including genetic influences and situational factors. P.F. Strawson used reactive attitudes to ground responsibility within social practices. Daniel Dennett suggested responsibility might be a purely metaphysical concern.

Collective responsibility extends to groups and corporations. The question of whether AI and robots can be morally responsible remains debated, with arguments about intentionality, consciousness, and accountability gaps.

Related concepts include accountability, blame, duty, legal liability, moral agency, moral hazard, punishment, free will, determinism, and social responsibility.
